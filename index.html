Formula Student front wing aerodynamics

STAR-CCM+
Steady RANS

Role: geometry cleanup, meshing & setup

Parametric study of a multi-element front wing to maximize front axle downforce at acceptable drag levels. Included mesh independence, y+ control, and correlation against a reference baseline.

External aero k-ω SST Mesh independence

The goal of this study was to quantify how front wing geometry influences balance and overall aerodynamic efficiency of a Formula Student car. Several configurations were explored (variation in camber, AoA, and endplate design) under a range of ride heights.

Key aspects of the setup:

  • Cleaned and defeatured CAD from the FS chassis team.
  • Hybrid prism / polyhedral mesh with systematic y+ checks.
  • k–ω SST model with steady RANS, pressure-based solver.
  • Mesh independence study on 3 mesh densities.

The final configuration improved front axle downforce by ~18% while adding only ~6% drag compared to the baseline. This allowed a more forward aero balance, helping tyre utilization without excessively penalizing straight-line speed.

LES of cylinder wake at Re = 105

OpenFOAM
LES (WALE)

Role: solver setup & validation

Transient LES capturing vortex shedding and wake dynamics. Strouhal number, drag coefficient, and base pressure compared with literature and experiments to assess model performance.

LES Turbulence modeling Time-resolved data

This classic benchmark was used to verify both numerical settings and the chosen subgrid-scale model. The mesh was designed to have sufficient resolution in the shear layers and wake region, while remaining affordable for long time-series.

Highlights:

  • WALE SGS model with second-order accurate schemes.
  • Time step based on CFL < 0.5 in the shear layer region.
  • Monitoring of Cd, Cl and base pressure to ensure statistical convergence.

The predicted Strouhal number and mean drag coefficient matched reference values within a few percent, giving confidence in the LES setup and filters for future transient projects.

Intake manifold pressure loss analysis

STAR-CCM+
Steady RANS

Role: parametric design & CFD

Internal flow study of an intake manifold to reduce separation and improve flow uniformity. Geometry variants evaluated based on pressure loss and mass flow distribution across runners.

Internal flow Pressure loss Design iterations

The baseline manifold showed strong separation at the plenum entrance and uneven distribution between runners, leading to cylinder-to-cylinder imbalances. Several baffle and diffuser concepts were evaluated.

Metrics tracked:

  • Total pressure loss between inlet and runner exits.
  • Flow uniformity index for all runners.
  • Local Mach number and recirculation regions.

The optimized design reduced pressure loss by ~12% and improved mass flow uniformity significantly, giving a more consistent air delivery without increasing packaging complexity.

2D unsteady CFD solver (FVM, C++)

C++
In-house code

Role: numerical implementation

In-house 2D unsteady finite volume solver for incompressible Navier–Stokes equations. Staggered grid, pressure–velocity coupling, second-order schemes, and time integration tested on canonical flows.

Code / tools FVM C++

The solver was built to better understand how discretization choices impact stability and accuracy. It includes modular components for time integration, linear solvers, and boundary conditions.

Implemented features:

  • Staggered grid arrangement for pressure–velocity coupling.
  • Second-order upwind and central differencing schemes.
  • Pressure correction loop for incompressibility.

Validation was carried out on lid-driven cavity and fully developed channel flow, comparing velocity profiles and drag with literature and analytical solutions.

Cooling duct CHT optimization

STAR-CCM+
CHT

Role: setup & optimization

Conjugate heat transfer analysis of an electronics cooling duct, exploring trade-offs between maximum component temperature and pressure drop through automated design sweeps.

Heat transfer Optimization

The system combined solid and fluid regions to capture conduction, convection, and contact resistances. Several fin layouts, duct shapes, and flow rates were compared.

The design space was explored via:

  • Automated parametric sweeps driven by design tables.
  • Monitoring of key temperatures and pressure drop.
  • Post-processing templates to compare variants consistently.

The final design reduced peak component temperature by ~9 K while keeping the pressure drop within the fan’s operating limits.

Interactive D2Q9 Lattice Boltzmann Solver

JavaScript
Real-time CFD

Role: numerical implementation & visualization

Browser-based implementation of the D2Q9 Lattice Boltzmann Method for incompressible flow simulation. Run live simulations with adjustable parameters and multiple geometries—no installation required.

LBM Interactive WebGL/Canvas

This is a fully functional Lattice Boltzmann Method solver running entirely in the browser. The D2Q9 lattice scheme solves the incompressible Navier–Stokes equations using kinetic theory and local collision operators.

Key features:

  • D2Q9 lattice with BGK collision operator.
  • Bounce-back boundary conditions for solid walls and objects.
  • Real-time visualization of velocity, vorticity, or pressure fields.
  • Multiple geometries: cylinder, airfoil (NACA 0012), square, flat plate, triangle.
  • Interactive parameter control: inlet velocity, kinematic viscosity.
  • Pure JavaScript implementation—no external CFD libraries.

The solver updates at interactive frame rates, allowing you to explore how flow behaves around different shapes. Watch vortex shedding develop behind a cylinder or see boundary layer separation on an airfoil—all computed in real time.
